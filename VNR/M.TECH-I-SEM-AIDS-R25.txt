VNR VIGNANA JYOTHI INSTITUTE OF ENGINEERING AND TECHNOLOGY
M.Tech. I Semester
(25PC1AS01) ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE AND INTELLIGENT SYSTEMS
TEACHING SCHEME EVALUATION SCHEME
L T/P C SE CA ELA SEE TOTAL
3 0 3 30 5 5 60 100
COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To introduce key AI problems, complexity classes, and AI paradigms
• To explore fundamental search strategies and heuristics in problem solving
• To enable logical reasoning and planning in AI systems
• To examine learning methods and natural language processing techniques
• To present agent architectures and applications of intelligent systems
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: Identify major AI problems and distinguish AI paradigms
CO-2: Apply search strategies and heuristics to solve problems
CO-3: Use logic and planning techniques for knowledge representation
CO-4: Implement AI learning and NLP models for data analysis
CO-5: Design agent-based and intelligent systems for real-world applications
UNIT-I:
Introduction: Overview of AI problems, AI problems as NP, NP-Complete and NP Hard
problems.
Strong and weak, neat and scruffy, symbolic and sub-symbolic, knowledge-based
and data-driven AI.
UNIT-II:
Search Strategies: Problem spaces (states, goals and operators), problem solving by
search, Heuristics and informed search, Min-max Search, Alpha-beta pruning.
Constraint satisfaction (backtracking and local search methods).
UNIT-III:
Knowledge representation and reasoning: Propositional and predicate logic,
Resolution and theorem proving, Temporal and spatial reasoning. Probabilistic
reasoning, Bayes theorem. Totally ordered and partially-ordered Planning. Goal stack
planning, Nonlinear planning, Hierarchical planning
UNIT-IV:
Learning: Learning from example, Learning by advice, Explanation based learning,
Learning in problem solving, Classification, Inductive learning, Naive Bayesian
Classifier, decision trees.
Natural Language Processing: Language models, n-grams, Vector space models, Bag
of words, Text classification. Information retrieval.
UNIT-V:
Agents: Definition of agents, Agent architectures (e.g., reactive, layered, cognitive),
Multi-agent systems- Collaborating agents, Competitive agents, Swarm systems and
biologically inspired models.
Intelligent Systems: Representing and Using Domain Knowledge, Expert System Shells,
Explanation, Knowledge Acquisition.
Key Application Areas: Expert system, decision support systems, Speech and vision,
Natural language processing, Information Retrieval, Semantic Web.
TEXT BOOK:
1. Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach by S. Russell and P. Norvig, Prentice Hall
REFERENCES:
1. Artificial Intelligence by Elaine Rich, Kevin Knight and Shivashankar B Nair, Tata
McGraw-Hill
2. Introduction to Artificial Intelligence and Expert Systems by Dan W. Patterson,
Pearson Education

(25PC1AS02) STATISTICAL FOUNDATIONS FOR DATA SCIENCE
TEACHING SCHEME EVALUATION SCHEME
L T/P C SE CA ELA SEE TOTAL
3 0 3 30 5 5 60 100
COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To introduce the fundamentals of data, probability, and various probability
distributions used in data analysis
• To make statistical inferences about data for predictive modelling
• To learn essential linear algebra concepts and their applications in data science
and dimensionality reduction
• To explore the principles of optimization and their role in solving machine learning
and data science problems
• To study dimensionality reduction using PCA and analyze stochastic processes and
Markov models in data science
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: Understand data Science and basics of various distributions
CO-2: Perform statistical inference and regression analysis for data-driven decision-
making
CO-3: Understand and apply the basic mathematical and statistical concepts in data
science
CO-4: Optimize the available data using certain optimization techniques
CO-5: Able to simplify the complexity in high-dimensional data while retaining trends
and interpret complex data patterns
UNIT-I:
What is Data Science? Three Pillars of Data Science, Types of Data and Data
Representation, Random Variables and Probability Distributions, Discrete Distributions:
Binomial, Poisson, Continuous Distributions: Uniform, Normal, Exponential, Lognormal,
Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)
UNIT-II:
Descriptive and Inferential Statistics, Measures of Central Tendency, Dispersion,
Skewness, Kurtosis, Sampling and Sampling Distributions, Central Limit Theorem and its
Applications, Simple Linear Regression and Correlation, Least Squares Estimation and
Model Fitting, Inferences concerning Regression Coefficients and Predictions
UNIT-III:
Matrices and Systems of Linear Equations, Fields, Vector Spaces, Linear
Independence, Basis, Dimension, Rank, Null Space, Rank-Nullity Theorem, Linear
Transformations and their Properties, Norms and Inner Products, Orthogonality and
Orthonormal Bases, Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors.
UNIT-IV:
Fundamentals of optimization, components of optimization problems, types of
optimization problems, univariate optimization and related numerical examples,
multivariate optimization and related numerical examples. Gradient Descent and its
Variants, Applications of Optimization.
UNIT-V:
Principal Component Analysis (PCA): Motivation and Geometric Intuition, Distance
Minimization and Variance Maximization, Eigenvalue Decomposition and PCA
Implementation, PCA for Dimensionality Reduction, Visualization using PCA (e.g.,
MNIST dataset), Limitations of PCA.
Stochastic Processes and Markov Chains: Introduction to Stochastic Processes and
Markov Processes, Applications of Markov Analysis in Data Science.
TEXT BOOKS:
1. Ronald E. Walpole, Raymond H. Myers, Sharon L. Myers, Keying Ye, Probability &
Statistics for Engineers & Scientists, 9th Ed. Pearson Publishers
2. Probability and Mathematical Statistics, Prasanna Sahoo, 2008
3. Linear Algebra and Optimization for Machine Learning, Charu C. Aggarwal,
Springer
REFERENCES:
1. Computational and Inferential Thinking: The Foundations of Data Science, Adi
Adhikari and John De Nero, 1st Edition, 2019
1. Doing Data Science, Straight Talk from The Frontline, Cathy O’Neil and Rachel
Schutt, O’Reilly, 2014
2. Mathematical Foundations of Data Science Using R, Matthias Dehmer, Salissou
Moutari, Frank Emmert-Streib, De Gruyter Oldenbourg, 2020.
4. Probability and Statistics for Data Science: Math + R + Data, Norman Matloff, CRC
Data Science Series, 2019
5. Introduction to Linear Algebra- by Gilbert Strang

(25PC1CP02) ADVANCED DATA STRUCTURES AND ALGORITHMS
TEACHING SCHEME EVALUATION SCHEME
L T/P C SE CA ELA SEE TOTAL
3 0 3 30 5 5 60 100
COURSE PRE-REQUISITES: Basic knowledge on Data Structures and Algorithm Design
and Analysis
COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To introduce the advanced methods of designing and analyzing algorithms
• To choose appropriate algorithms and use it for a specific problem
• To familiarize basic paradigms and data structures used to solve advanced
algorithmic problems
• To understand different classes of problems concerning their computation
difficulties
• To impart principles of good program design, particularly the effective use of data
abstraction.
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: Understand different advanced data structures and classes of problems
concerning their computation difficulties.
CO-2: Analyze the complexity/performance of different algorithms and data
structures operations.
CO-3: Categorize the different problems in various classes according to their
complexity
CO-4: Identify appropriate data structure and algorithms design technique and use it
for a specific problem solving
CO-5: Apply advanced problem-solving skills in various domains
UNIT-I:
Abstract Data Type: Priority Queue, Min/Max Heap, Binomial Heaps, Fibonacci heaps.
Hashing: Implementation of Dictionaries, Review of Hashing, Hash Function, Collision
Resolution Techniques in Hashing, Separate Chaining, Open Addressing, Linear
Probing, Quadratic Probing, Double Hashing, Rehashing, Extendible Hashing.
Counting and radix sorting techniques.
Trees: AVL Trees, Red Black Trees, B-Trees, Tries.
UNIT-II:
Text Processing: Sting Operations, Brute-Force Pattern Matching, the Boyer-Moore
Algorithm, the Knuth-Morris -Pratt Algorithm, the Longest Common Subsequence
Problem (LCS), Applying Dynamic Programming to the LCS Problem. Introduction to
Convex Hull, finding the convex hull and Graham scan approach.
UNIT-III:
Graph: Definitions and Elementary Algorithms: Shortest Path in Graphs: BFS, Dijkasra's,
Bellman-Ford, Floyd-Warshall algorithm, DFS, topological sorting amortized analysis.
Modulo Representation of Integers/Polynomials: Chinese Remainder Theorem,
Conversion between base-representation and modulo-representation. Polynomial
addition and multiplication.
UNIT-IV:
Flow-Networks: Maxflow-min cut theorem, Ford-Fulkerson Method to compute
maximum flow, Edmond-Karp maximum-flow algorithm.
Matrix Computations: Stassen’s algorithm and introduction to divide and conquer
paradigm, inverse of a triangular matrix, LUP-decomposition.
UNIT-V:
Linear Programming: Geometry of the feasibility region and Simplex algorithm.
NP Hard and NP-Complete problems: P, NP, NP-complete and NP-hard. Cook’s
theorem. Recent Trends in problem solving paradigms using recent searching and
sorting techniques by applying recently proposed data structures.
TEXTBOOKS:
1. Data Structures and Algorithm Analysis in C++, Mark Allen Weiss, 2nd Edition,
Pearson, 2004
2. Introduction to Algorithms, Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein
3. Fundamentals of Computer Algorithms, E. Horrowitz, S. Sahni, S. Rajasekaran, 2nd
Edition, University Press, 2007
REFERENCES:
1. Algorithm Design – Foundations, Analysis, and Internet Algorithms, M. T. Goodrich,
R. Tomassia, John Wiley & Sons, 2002
2. The Design and Analysis of Computer Algorithms, Aho, Hopcroft, Ullman
3. Algorithm Design, Kleinberg and Tardos
4. Design Analysis and Algorithms, Hari Mohan Pandy, University Science Press, 2009

2. https://www.ics.uci.edu/~eppstein/161/960312.html

COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To understand data architecture, sources, management, and preprocessing
techniques for analytical readiness
• To learn data analytics tools, modeling techniques, and their applications in
business contexts
• To study regression and logistic regression concepts for building predictive
analytical models
• To explore segmentation methods, supervised and unsupervised learning, and
time series forecasting approaches
• To understand and apply data visualization techniques for interpreting and
communicating analytical insights effectively
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: To design and manage data architectures, ensuring data quality and
consistency
CO-2: To apply data analytics and modeling techniques using appropriate tools for
business decision-making
CO-3: To develop and evaluate regression-based models for prediction and analysis
CO-4: To implement segmentation and time series models for forecasting and pattern
recognition
CO-5: To create meaningful visual representations of complex datasets using
advanced visualization methods
UNIT-I:
Data Management: Design Data Architecture and manage the data for analysis,
understand various sources of Data like Sensors/Signals/GPS etc. Data Management,
Data Quality(noise, outliers, missing values, duplicate data) and Data Processing &
Processing.
UNIT-II:
Data Analytics: Introduction to Analytics, Introduction to Tools and Environment,
Application of Modeling in Business, Databases & Types of Data and Variables, Data
Modeling Techniques, Missing Imputations etc. Need for Business Modeling.
UNIT-III:
Regression – Concepts, Blue property assumptions, Least Square Estimation, Variable
Rationalization, and Model Building etc.
Logistic Regression: Model Theory, Model fit Statistics, Model Construction, Analytics
applications to various Business Domains etc.
UNIT-IV:
Object Segmentation: Regression Vs Segmentation – Supervised and Unsupervised
Learning, Tree Building – Regression, Classification, Overfitting, Pruning and
Complexity, Multiple Decision Trees etc.
Time Series Methods: Arima, Measures of Forecast Accuracy, STL approach, Extract
features from generated model as Height, Average Energy etc and Analyze for
prediction
UNIT-V:
Data Visualization: Pixel-Oriented Visualization Techniques, Geometric Projection
Visualization Techniques, Icon-Based Visualization Techniques, Hierarchical
Visualization Techniques, Visualizing Complex Data and Relations.

COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To introduce the fundamental ideas of software agents and Agentic AI
• To explain how agents perceive, reason, and act in different environments
• To develop basic skills to design single-agent and multi-agent solutions
• To provide hands-on exposure to open-source frameworks for building simple
agents
• To emphasize responsible, ethical and safe deployment of agent-based systems
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: Define the basic concepts and characteristics of autonomous agents
CO-2: Explain the structure and behaviour of simple agent architectures
CO-3: Apply interaction, coordination and task-allocation concepts in basic multi-
agent systems
CO-4: Use beginner-friendly tools to implement simple agentic applications
CO-5: Identify ethical and safety issues in the use of agent-based technologies
UNIT-I:
Introduction to Agentic AI: Evolution of AI from expert systems to Agentic AI, Agents
vs. traditional programs, Characteristics of agents: autonomy, pro-activeness,
reactivity, social ability, Types of agents: reflex-based, goal-based, utility-based,
learning agents, Agents and their environments: deterministic vs. stochastic, discrete
vs. continuous, static vs. dynamic, Illustrative applications: chatbots, shopping
assistants, route-planning agents.
UNIT-II:
Agent Architectures and Reasoning: Basic agent architectures: reactive, deliberative,
hybrid Perception–action cycle in agents, State representation and simple reasoning,
Concept of goal-directed behaviour, Introduction to planning at a conceptual level,
Basics of learning in agents (overview of reinforcement learning concepts).
UNIT-III:
Multi-Agent Systems (MAS): Need for multi-agent systems; examples in everyday life,
Communication among agents: simple message passing, Cooperation and
competition; basic coordination strategies, Introduction to task allocation and
resource sharing, Simple MAS scenarios: traffic-signal control, team-based robotics.
UNIT-IV:
Tools and Applications of Agentic AI: Overview of current agentic toolkits and
frameworks: NetLogo, JADE, LangChain, CrewAI, AutoGPT (conceptual level only),
Building simple personal-assistant or information-retrieval agents, Agents with LLM
support – introductory prompting and workflow chaining, Agents in healthcare,
education, customer support, and research assistance.
UNIT-V:
Safety, Ethics and Future Directions: Importance of transparency, fairness and
accountability in agentic systems, Basic safety measures: controllability, human-in-
the-loop design, Data privacy and security issues in autonomous agents, Limitations
of current agentic approaches, Future scope: integration with IoT, robotics and cloud
platforms, Research opportunities for entry-level graduate students.

2022

sections)

(25PE1AS04) AIML TECHNIQUES FOR CYBER SECURITY
TEACHING SCHEME EVALUATION SCHEME
L T/P C SE CA ELA SEE TOTAL
3 0 3 30 5 5 60 100
COURSE OBJECTIVES:
• To introduce the fundamental principles of Artificial Intelligence and Machine
Learning in the context of cyber security
• To develop understanding of machine learning algorithms for intrusion detection,
malware analysis, and threat classification.
• To explore deep learning and neural network applications in security analytics
• To familiarize students with adversarial machine learning and its implications in
cyber defence
• To enable learners to analyse, design, and implement AI-based systems for
detecting and preventing cyber-attacks
COURSE OUTCOMES: After completion of the course, the student should be able to
CO-1: Explain the role of AI and ML in solving modern cyber security challenges
CO-2: Apply supervised and unsupervised ML algorithms for intrusion and anomaly
detection
CO-3: Utilize deep learning architectures for malware detection, spam filtering, and
phishing prevention
CO-4: Evaluate cyber threats using AI-driven models and interpret results with
explainable AI approaches
CO-5: Design and implement intelligent cyber defense mechanisms using ML and
adversarial learning concepts
UNIT-I:
Introduction to AI & ML in Cyber Security: Overview of Cyber Security threats and
challenges. Role of AI and ML in Cyber Defence. Basic AI concepts: Knowledge
representation, reasoning, and learning. Introduction to Machine Learning:
Supervised, Unsupervised, and Reinforcement Learning. Need for AI and ML in modern
cyber defence systems.
UNIT-II:
Machine Learning Techniques for Threat Detection: Basics of Machine Learning: Data
pre-processing, encoding, feature selection, and normalization. Supervised Learning:
Decision Trees, Random Forest, Naïve Bayes, SVM, KNN. Unsupervised Learning:

CO-5: Understand that IPR protection provides an incentive to inventors for further
research work and investment in R & D, which leads to creation of new and better
products, and in turn brings about, economic growth and social benefits
UNIT-I:
Meaning of research problem, Sources of research problem, Criteria Characteristics
of a good research problem, Errors in selecting a research problem, Scope and
objectives of research problem.
Approaches of investigation of solutions for research problem, data collection,
analysis, interpretation, Necessary instrumentations
UNIT-II:
Effective literature studies approaches, analysis, Plagiarism, Research ethics
UNIT-III:
Effective technical writing, how to write report, Paper Developing a Research
Proposal, Format of research proposal, a presentation and assessment by a review
committee
UNIT-IV:
Nature of Intellectual Property: Patents, Designs, Trade and Copyright. Process of
Patenting and Development: technological research, innovation, patenting,
development. International Scenario: International cooperation on Intellectual
Property. Procedure for grants of patents, Patenting under PCT.
UNIT-V:
Patent Rights: Scope of Patent Rights. Licensing and transfer of technology. Patent
information and databases. Geographical Indications. New Developments in IPR:
Administration of Patent System.
New developments in IPR; IPR of Biological Systems, Computer Software etc.
Traditional knowledge Case Studies, IPR and IITs
